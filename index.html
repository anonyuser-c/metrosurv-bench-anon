MetroSurv-Bench

A benchmark for multimodal traffic surveillance understanding, temporal grounding, and multi-intersection reasoning.

Paper (Anonymous) Dataset (Anonymous) Code (Anonymous)

Author names, affiliations, and external repository links are intentionally omitted for double-blind review.

Abstract

MetroSurv-Bench is a benchmark for evaluating whether modern multimodal large language models can handle surveillance-oriented traffic understanding beyond generic video QA. The benchmark jointly measures traffic element perception, dynamic event understanding, temporal grounding, and multi-intersection reasoning across upstream and downstream camera views. It is designed to expose capability gaps that are easy to miss when evaluation is limited to short, single-view, or purely recognition-centric video tasks.

What MetroSurv-Bench Evaluates

Level 1

Traffic element perception, including pedestrians and traffic officers, vehicles, road infrastructure, and counting.

Level 2

Dynamic event understanding, including single-vehicle behavior, inter-vehicle interaction, traffic-flow behavior, and dynamic counting.

Level 3

Multi-intersection reasoning, including lane correspondence, timestamp-based re-identification, destination tracking, and congestion causation.

Temporal Grounding

Natural-language localization of traffic events with mIoU and recall at multiple temporal IoU thresholds.

Benchmark Taxonomy

The benchmark is organized around a three-level hierarchy for surveillance QA, plus a dedicated temporal grounding track.

Example Tasks

MetroSurv-Bench covers traffic element perception, dynamic event understanding, and cross-camera reasoning between upstream and downstream intersections.

Dataset Composition

632 MCQ samples
62 Multi-intersection samples
364 Temporal grounding samples
1,058 Total benchmark instances

The dataset is not restricted to very short clips. MCQ is concentrated in the 0-60s range, while temporal grounding is distributed across 0-30s, 30-60s, 1-2min, and 2-5min. The multi-intersection subset contains a relatively larger share of long-horizon examples.

Main Findings

  • Commercial models remain strongest overall. Gemini-3.1-Pro-Preview achieves the best reported overall score among fully evaluated models on the main benchmark (66.3).
  • Open-source models are already competitive on Level 1 perception. Qwen3.5-27B reaches the best Level 1 average (79.2), suggesting that basic perception is no longer the primary bottleneck.
  • Cross-camera reasoning is much harder. Multi-intersection reasoning produces a sharper model separation than single-view perception and event recognition.
  • Grounding rankings differ from QA rankings. TimeLens is highly competitive on temporal localization, indicating that video-specialized modeling particularly helps grounding.
